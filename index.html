About

Why I do this

My students are working adults, parents, veterans, and first-generation college students. Government already shapes their lives every single day — they deserve a course that treats them like citizens, not test-takers.

On set, filming GOVT 2305 · Take 1

I teach GOVT 2305 and 2306 — Federal and Texas Government — in Fort Worth, where I grew tired of watching capable, busy people disengage from courses built on memorization and Scantrons. So I started replacing exams with real civic action: attending public meetings, contacting representatives, mapping public services, showing up.

Everything I build is shared as a free, open educational resource. If you're an instructor, take it, adapt it, and make it yours. If you're a student, these tools were built for you first.

“Government is a place where people can come together to solve problems.”

— Stephanie Stewart, my mom

She means it, too. In November 1984 — nine months pregnant with me, days before I was born — she stood in line for hours to cast her vote. Reagan won anyway, to her lasting disgust. She carried me to the polls before I could walk there myself, and years later she stood right beside me for my first legal vote.

She taught me that long before any professor did — and she lives it: hardworking, empathetic, civic-minded. Everything on this page is built in her honor.

Flagship Project

The Civic Action Project (CAP)

My signature framework — and the subject of my doctoral research. CAP replaces traditional government exams with a menu of 39 hands-on civic engagement activities, from attending city council meetings to building public service directories. Includes complete instructor resources, rubrics, tracking tools, and student outcome data.

Texas In Action (TIA)

The Lone Star counterpart to CAP — civic engagement built specifically for Texas Government (GOVT 2306). Students connect directly with the state and local institutions that shape daily life in Texas, from the Capitol in Austin to their own county courthouse.

Federal Government Trailblazer Trek

Sixteen gamified, interactive learning modules covering all of American federal government. Students earn points, unlock reveal cards, pass knowledge checks, and generate integrity-coded completion reports — entirely in the browser, with zero tracking.

GOVT 2305 For Students 16 Modules

Texas Government Trailblazer Trek

Fifteen interactive modules on the same engine, rebuilt for Texas government — the Texas Constitution, the Legislature, the plural executive, and local government. Adds written reflection checkpoints so students connect each chapter to their own communities.

Keep Your Seat — Texas State Senate Simulation

Students serve as Texas State Senators, navigating committee assignments, floor votes, party pressure, and constituent demands — and discover firsthand why governing means making trade-offs. Built for the classroom: project it, play it, debrief it.

Proof of Concept

What showing up looks like

Lightly edited excerpts from real CAP submissions and student messages, shared with permission.

“Now I am invested”

A student sat in the City Hall parking lot before her first Fort Worth work session, watching police cars arrive and wondering what she’d signed up for. A councilmember walked her in. Two hours later, she’d seen a $79.5 million tax-abatement pitch, a bond-program debate, and a live lesson in federalism over state funding strings. Her verdict: “I probably would’ve gone my whole life and never made it to a government meeting. Now I am invested.”

Government Meeting Attendance · GOVT 2306

The business card

At her first Euless City Council meeting, a nervous student was personally welcomed by the Mayor Pro Tem — who handed her his business card and told her not to hesitate if she ever needed help. She stayed for drainage ordinances and utility-rate hearings, and left with a better definition than the textbook’s: civic engagement isn’t just voting — it’s going to a meeting, listening, and learning.

Government Meeting Attendance · GOVT 2305

The first FOIA

Investigating Fort Worth’s billion-dollar Panther Island project, a student filed his first public-records request with the water district, interviewed family about rising water bills, and delivered a lessons-learned report on oversight and transparency that wouldn’t look out of place on a consultant’s letterhead.

Government Investigation · GOVT 2306
The Approach

Built on three convictions

Choice drives motivation

Students engage when they have real autonomy. CAP offers dozens of paths to the same goal, so a night-shift nurse and an 18-year-old gamer can both find activities that fit their lives.

Experience beats memorization

You don't learn government from a Scantron. You learn it by sitting in a council meeting, emailing your representative, and seeing how the machine actually moves.

Good ideas should travel

Everything here is open educational material. The whole point is for other instructors to take it, adapt it, and put it in front of more students than I ever could alone.

Off the Clock

Beyond the classroom

The Stew abides. My path to academia was long, winding, and loud. Before heading to the University of Washington for a graduate degree in Political Science, I was a professional musician, parlaying guitar skills I've honed over 25-plus years. I was fortunate to play with and for some great people — and I learned a lot about entertaining a disengaged crowd. I don't perform publicly much anymore, but I co-sponsor the TCC Trinity River Guitar Club, which lets me watch the next generation of rockstars up close. If I'm not on campus, there's a good chance I have a guitar in my hands, annoying my basset hounds — Sadie Mae and Sammie Doo — while they try to sleep. Jam!

I'm married to Dr. Mikaela Stewart, Associate Professor of Biochemistry at TCU, and we have an eight-year-old son, Levi — yes, the same Levi behind the Wheel of Levi in the games library. He remains my most demanding user and my best sidekick. I work for him now.
