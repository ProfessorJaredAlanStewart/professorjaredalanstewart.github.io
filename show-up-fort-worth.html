A Field Guide

Show Up in Fort Worth

Where the meetings are, how to speak, how to get the pothole on your street fixed, and how to make sure your vote counts. Everything on this page is free, public, and yours by right.

Every institution on this page belongs to you. You don't need an appointment, an invitation, or a reason — being a resident is the whole credential. Most of my students who attend their first public meeting say some version of the same thing afterward: "I had no idea I was allowed to just... walk in." You are. Here's exactly how.

The law is on your side. The Texas Open Meetings Act requires governmental bodies to meet in public, post their agendas at least 72 hours in advance, and let you watch every deliberation and vote. The Texas Public Information Act gives you the right to request government records. Showing up isn't a privilege they extend — it's an obligation they owe.

Attend a Fort Worth City Council meeting

City

This is where your property taxes, your streets, your water rates, your parks, and your police budget get decided. Council meets in the new City Hall — the converted Pier 1 tower by the Trinity River — in a chamber built with 250 seats specifically so the public can attend.

Where
Council Chamber, Fort Worth City Hall, 100 Fort Worth Trail, Fort Worth, TX 76102 (just right of the main entrance)
When
Tuesdays, roughly three per month — daytime meetings typically start at 10 a.m. and evening meetings at 6 p.m. Check the city meeting calendar for the exact schedule and posted agenda.
To speak
Fort Worth uses online Speaker/Comment Cards attached to each posted agenda. The path: go to fortworthtexas.gov, scroll to the City Council links in the site footer, and open "City Council Meeting and Work Session Agendas." Search the calendar (set it to All Departments if nothing appears), open the Agenda PDF for your meeting, find the Speaker/Comment Card link under the agenda item you want to address — or under the public comment section for anything else — and fill out the card. As of 2026, speakers get three minutes, and residents fought to win back open comment at the end of regular meetings this year; use it. Step-by-step screenshots are on the City Secretary's instructions page, and the office will walk you through it at 817-392-6150.
Can't make it?
Every meeting streams live and is archived on FWTV and the city's YouTube channel. Watching counts as showing up for CAP purposes — but the room hits different.
Your district
Fort Worth has 10 council districts plus a citywide mayor. Find yours — and your council member's contact info — at the city council page.
Pro tip: Read the agenda the day before and pick one item to follow. A two-hour meeting becomes a story when you're rooting for (or against) something specific — and locating the Speaker/Comment Card the night before beats hunting for it from the chamber lobby. Bonus: the consent agenda — dozens of items passed in a single vote — is where most of the actual money moves.

Attend Tarrant County Commissioners Court

County

The Commissioners Court — a county judge elected countywide plus four commissioners elected by precinct — runs the county: the hospital district, the jail, county roads, elections, and a multi-billion-dollar budget. Despite the name, it's a governing body, not a courtroom.

Where
Tarrant County Administration Building, 100 E. Weatherford St., Fort Worth, TX 76196 — downtown, about a mile from City Hall
When
Monthly, generally the second Tuesday at 10 a.m., with special meetings as needed. (The court cut back from weekly meetings in late 2025 — a controversial change worth discussing in class.) Agendas post at least three business days ahead at the county's agenda page.
To speak
Once the agenda posts, register online via the "Register to Speak" buttons in the posted agenda, or in person at the Administration Building, Suite 404. Deadlines are strict — check the posted agenda for the cutoff. You can also submit written comments on any agenda item.
Can't make it?
Meetings stream live on the county's YouTube channel; recordings post within about a day.
Pro tip: County government is the layer almost nobody watches, which makes it the layer where one informed resident stands out the most. The public comment rules here are stricter than the city's — read the rules of decorum before you go, and watch the clock when you speak.

Attend a Fort Worth ISD board meeting

School District

School boards decide budgets, school closures, curriculum policy, and superintendent hires — and right now, Fort Worth ISD is one of the most consequential civics case studies in Texas: in March 2026, the Texas Education Agency suspended the elected Board of Trustees' authority and appointed a Board of Managers to govern the district. State power versus local control isn't a textbook hypothetical here. It's Tuesday night.

Where
FWISD District Service Center, 7060 Camp Bowie Blvd., Fort Worth, TX 76116
When
Check the FWISD board meetings page for the calendar and posted agendas.
To speak
After the agenda posts, call 817-814-1920 by 4 p.m. the day of the meeting to sign up, or sign up in person at the meeting (arrive early — in-person signup closes before the meeting starts). Written comments can be emailed to boardmeetings-publiccomment@fwisd.org by noon the day of the meeting.
Can't make it?
Meetings stream live on the Fort Worth ISD YouTube channel and the district's Video on Demand page.
Pro tip: Live outside FWISD? Every school district in Tarrant County — Keller, Crowley, Arlington, Eagle Mountain-Saginaw, and the rest — holds public board meetings under the same Open Meetings Act rules. Your district's website will have the schedule.

Get a pothole fixed (and report everything else)

City

This is the single fastest way to watch your government work for you. Fort Worth's street crews fix roughly a hundred potholes a day, and they aim to repair reported ones within about 48 hours. But here's the catch, straight from the crews themselves: if nobody reports it, they don't know it's there.

Best way
The MyFW app (free on the App Store and Google Play). Snap a photo, drop a map pin, submit. You can track the request's status and get a notification when it's done.
Other ways
Call the city's customer care line at 817-392-1234, text "hello" to 817-835-6939 (MYFW), or submit a request on the city website.
Also works for
Broken traffic signals, missing signs, streetlight outages, graffiti, high grass, illegal dumping, code violations — anything that's the city's job to fix.
Pro tip: Note the exact location and rough size when you report. And screenshot your request — a before/after with the city's "completed" notification is a perfect artifact for a CAP write-up, and an oddly satisfying superpower in general.

Register to vote (or make sure you still are)

County

Texas does not have online voter registration for new voters — it's paper, and there's a hard deadline of 30 days before any election you want to vote in. That trips up more of my students than any other rule on this page. Don't be one of them.

Check first
Look yourself up at the Texas Secretary of State's "Am I Registered?" portal. Moved or changed your name? You can update an existing registration online through Texas.gov.
To register
Pick up a postage-paid application at any Tarrant County library, post office, city hall, subcourthouse, or public high school — or print one from Tarrant County Elections — and mail it or drop it off at the Elections Center, 2700 Premier St., Fort Worth, TX 76111. Questions: 817-831-VOTE (8683).
Then vote
Tarrant County uses countywide vote centers — on Election Day you can vote at any center in the county, not just your precinct. Early voting runs for about two weeks before most elections. Locations and sample ballots: Tarrant County Elections.
Pro tip: The elections nobody shows up for — municipal races, school board, bond packages, constitutional amendments — are decided by a tiny fraction of registered voters. That's not a depressing fact; it's an arbitrage opportunity. Your vote is mathematically never worth more than in a May local election.

Find and contact your state representatives

State

Austin decides more about your daily life than Washington does — your tuition, your driver's license, your criminal code, your property tax system. You have one state representative and one state senator, and their offices answer constituent mail because constituents are the whole job.

Who are they?
Enter your address at Who Represents Me? — the official Texas Capitol lookup. It returns your state rep, state senator, U.S. representative, and State Board of Education member, with contact links.
Track a bill
Texas Legislature Online lets you search any bill, read its text and history, and set up free email alerts when it moves. The Legislature meets in regular session for 140 days in odd-numbered years — the next regular session convenes in January 2027 — but interim committee hearings happen year-round and accept public testimony.
Make contact
A short, specific, polite email or call to the district office about one bill or one issue gets logged and tallied. Form letters get counted; personal stories get remembered.
Pro tip: Want to testify in Austin someday? Watch a committee hearing on Texas Legislature Online first — public testimony is usually two to three minutes, and you'll see that ordinary Texans do it every single day the Legislature is in session.

Request public records

Any Level

Under the Texas Public Information Act, you can request records from any Texas governmental body — emails, contracts, budgets, reports — and they generally must respond promptly, with most responses due within 10 business days. One of my students used this to investigate the billion-dollar Panther Island project. His first request. It worked.

City records
Fort Worth accepts public information requests through the City Secretary's office and its online records portal — start at the City Secretary page.
County records
Tarrant County's public information request process lives on tarrantcountytx.gov — search "public information request."
How to ask
Be specific and narrow: a date range, a department, a document type. "All emails about X between January and March" beats "everything about X." You don't have to explain why you want it — the law doesn't require a reason.
Pro tip: Before filing a request, check whether it's already public — council agendas, budgets, check registers, and crime data are often posted online. The best records request is the one you didn't have to file.

More ways in

All Levels

Showing up has more doors than a council chamber:

Serve on a board
Fort Worth has dozens of resident boards and commissions — zoning, parks, libraries, community development — and they regularly need volunteers. Browse openings and apply through the city's boards and commissions page. Yes, regular people get appointed. That's the point.
Answer jury duty
It's the one form of civic participation that comes with a summons — and it's the only time most citizens directly exercise judicial power. Don't dodge it; bring a book and pay attention. It's the best free civics seminar in the county.
Get a library card
The Fort Worth Public Library is free, and it's quietly one of the city's best civic institutions: meeting rooms, research databases, tax help, voter registration forms, and librarians who can find anything.
Join your neighborhood
Neighborhood associations are where city hall actually meets your street. The city's Neighborhood Services department keeps a directory — and if your neighborhood doesn't have one, starting one is itself a civic act.
Follow local news
You can't show up to what you don't know about. The Fort Worth Report (nonprofit, free) and KERA News cover city hall, the county, and the school district week in, week out.

Your first meeting: a checklist

Print this. Or don't. Just go.
  • Read the agenda the night before. They're posted at least 72 hours out, by law. Pick one item to follow.
  • Arrive 15 minutes early. Public buildings have security lines, and seats up front go first.
  • You don't have to speak. Sitting and watching is fully showing up. Nobody will call on you.
  • There's no dress code. Come from work, come from class, come as you are.
  • Take notes. Who voted how, what surprised you, what you'd ask. (My students: this is your CAP write-up.)
  • Introduce yourself to one person. A council member, a staffer, a fellow resident. Civic life runs on hello.

Meeting times, locations, and sign-up rules change — sometimes mid-year. Every entry above links to the official source; always check the posted agenda before you go. Last verified June 2026.
